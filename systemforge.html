⬠ System Forge · Design Principles

UX foundations for a SysML2 modeling tool — reducing cognitive load in B2B systems engineering software

2025–26 · Collaboration with Cauê Napier (Systems Engineer, SSC)

Context

System Forge is a model-based systems engineering (MBSE) tool in early development. Cauê Napier, systems engineer at the Swedish Space Corporation (SSC), is building it as a side project to make SysML2-based modeling more accessible. At this stage — core architecture taking shape — there's an opportunity to establish design principles before patterns solidify.

Framing and Reframing

Initial framing: Support the product with UX — review screens, improve layouts.

The reframe: Before reviewing screens, define what principles those screens should follow. SysML2 specifies semantics; presentation and interaction are left to tooling. That's the design space — mapping it was the first task.

Design Principles for System Forge

Synthesized from Cauê's product vision, UX research, and B2B domain considerations.

01
One Model, Many Views

Single source of truth. Diagram, tree, table, text all sync. Ref: Tufte "Escaping Flatland"

02
Consistent Visual Grammar

Same meaning = same appearance, everywhere. Ref: Bertin; Tufte "Small Multiples"

03
Progressive Disclosure

Show what the task requires, hide the rest. Ref: NNGroup; Miller's Law

04
Click Anywhere, Land Everywhere

Selection syncs across views. Ref: Linked views pattern

05
Context Preservation

Never "zoom in and get lost." Ref: Tufte "Micro/Macro"; Shneiderman

06
Type-First Organization

Group by type, not creation order. Ref: Information architecture

07
Edge Density Management

Limit or filter edges to reduce noise. Ref: Tufte "Layering"

08
Validation Timing

Real-time vs. on-demand error checking. Ref: B2B expert workflows

09
Safe Exploration

Generous undo, non-destructive defaults. Ref: Norman's DOET

10
Leverage Familiar Patterns

Build on IDE/CAD conventions. Ref: Jakob's Law

Reflection

On the work: SysML2 defines semantics; presentation is the tool's responsibility. Translating that into concrete guidance — "you can style this element, but this shape is fixed" — required working through the spec systematically.

On principles before pixels: The expectation for UX Designers is often visual output. But a dropdown without a principle is just a guess. Principles create constraints — and constraints make design decisions defensible, not arbitrary.
