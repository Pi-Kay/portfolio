Designing for Domains You Don't Know

What happens when "just apply UX rules" doesn't work — and how AI changes the learning process

Design process · B2B software · AI collaboration · 8 min read

I'm a designer working on a systems modeling tool for space engineers. The tool uses SysML v2 — a standardized notation for modeling complex systems. I had never used SysML before this project.

I previously worked at Valispace, a systems engineering tool for requirements and data management. SysML 2 graphs weren't part of that scope — but the experience informed my approach. I knew how engineers interact with complex tools, where usability gaps appear, and how hard it is to bridge design and engineering thinking.

When I started this project, someone suggested I could "just handle the visual/UX part" without needing deep domain knowledge. This is a common assumption. It's also wrong — at least for anything beyond surface-level fixes.

This article is about what I learned: why designing for specialized B2B software requires domain understanding, how AI can help bridge knowledge gaps without replacing expert collaboration, and why design and engineering are almost opposite thinking modes that need to meet somewhere in the middle.

• • •

The "just apply UX rules" problem

There's a persistent idea that UX design is a set of rules you apply to any interface. Check contrast ratios. Add undo buttons. Follow heuristics. Ship.

For surface fixes, this works. Color contrast doesn't require understanding the domain. Neither does adding keyboard shortcuts or improving error messages.

But for structural design decisions — what's most visible, what's hidden, how users progress through complexity — you need to understand the work the software supports. Otherwise you're flying blind.

Surface fixes vs. structural design require different levels of domain understanding

Consider a simple question: what should be most prominent in the interface? To answer that, you need to know what users do most often. You need to know what's done daily versus once a year. You need to know what mistakes are common and costly.

None of that comes from UX heuristics. It comes from understanding the domain.

The learning gap

So I needed to learn SysML v2. But how?

A SysML course assumes engineering background I don't have. It would teach me to use the notation — not to understand what visual decisions are standardized versus flexible, or where learnability problems come from.

Meanwhile, UX bootcamp methods assume consumer apps with quick user testing. "Talk to users early and often." Good advice — but space engineers aren't available for quick hallway tests. And asking them why SysML is hard to learn is useless if I don't understand what SysML is.

Neither standard path fits: engineering courses assume too much, UX methods assume the wrong context

These gaps are where a designer + engineer hybrid has the most impact.

I needed a different approach: learn enough to have informed conversations, without becoming an engineer. Understand what's standardized versus flexible. Know which questions to ask and which assumptions to question.

Where AI helps

This is where AI became genuinely useful — not as a replacement for expertise, but as a translation layer.

Plain language explanations. AI can explain engineering concepts without assuming prior knowledge. I can ask for metaphors, for simple examples, for the historical context of why something exists. An engineer might not appreciate that mode switch — they're trained to be precise, not pedagogical. AI adapts to individual cognitive styles more easily.

Framework cross-referencing. I needed to check if frameworks from one field (cognitive load theory, Bertin's visual variables, Tufte's information design principles) could apply to another (systems modeling). AI is fast at this kind of synthesis — generating connections I can then validate.

Distinguishing fixed from flexible. SysML v2 is a standard, but not everything in it is equally fixed. Some elements are semantic (what things mean) — those must be followed. Some are presentation (how things look) — those can be improved. AI helped me quickly map this distinction across dozens of symbols.

AI bridges cognitive styles: translating for the designer, providing input for the engineer to validate

Where AI fails

But there are clear limits.

Plausible-looking nonsense. AI can generate synthesis that looks good on first view but falls apart under expert scrutiny. If you don't regularly validate with someone who actually knows the domain, you'll drift into embarrassing territory without noticing.

Quickly forgotten. Quickly-generated content is quickly forgotten. If I don't slow down to process — sometimes with pencil and paper, no screens — the material doesn't stick. I lose control of the process. The output becomes impressive-looking noise rather than actual understanding.

Too much output. AI makes it easy to generate volume. But volume without digestion becomes overwhelming. It loses credibility. The challenge is steering the process, not just running it.

The balance

AI speeds up learning. But the slower work — digesting, questioning, validating with real practitioners — can't be skipped. Both sides of a collaboration save time using AI appropriately. But "appropriately" means knowing what to ask AI and what to ask an expert.

Engineering vs. design thinking

Working closely with engineers surfaced a difference I hadn't fully appreciated: engineering and design are almost opposite thinking modes.

Engineers divide and conquer. Find a solution to this piece. Execute. Move on. This produces precise, robust components. It also produces software with many features but no redesigned whole — each part solved, but the connections not considered together.

Designers connect parts. Consider all elements at once. Think about the user moving through the software while getting distracted. This produces coherent experiences but requires holding complexity without resolving it prematurely.

Two thinking modes: engineering solves sequentially, design considers connections simultaneously

Neither mode is wrong. Engineering's precision and process produce reliable systems. Design's holistic view produces coherent experiences. The challenge is finding where they meet.

In practice, this means: engineers may assume designers can "just do the visual part" because they've solved their part — the logic, the functionality. But the visual layer isn't separate. It emerges from understanding how all the parts connect from the user's perspective.

Why human factors can't be automated

If UX design were just rules and process, AI could replace it. Apply heuristics. Check accessibility. Done.

But "user experience" means considering how humans actually use software. And humans are messy.

  • Attention wanders. People get distracted mid-task.
  • Mistakes happen. They need to be detectable and reversible.
  • Individuals differ. A newbie today learns differently from a 30-year veteran.
  • Context changes. How people work this year is different from five years ago.

Human factors designers embrace this variability. That's the part that can't be automated — not because it's too complex, but because it's not rule-based. It requires judgment about messy, changing, contradictory human behavior.

Some UX work is rule-based and automatable. The human factors part isn't.

Users know what they want or don't want. They can point to problems. But they're not designers — they're not responsible for researching frameworks that might solve those problems. That's the designer's job: translating user frustration into structural solutions that the user couldn't have specified.

Context-dependent UX

Most UX methods developed for consumer apps. Mass market. Quick user testing. "Increase engagement."

That's one context. Specialized B2B software is another.

  • Users aren't available for quick hallway tests. A space engineer's time is expensive and scheduled.
  • Early interviews are less useful than interviews with concrete artifacts. Asking "what's hard about SysML?" is useless if you don't understand what SysML is.
  • "Engagement" isn't the goal. Professional users want efficiency, not stickiness.

Universal principles (Norman's affordances, error recovery, consistency) still apply. But the method has to fit the context.

UX methods developed for one end of this spectrum don't automatically work at the other

What I actually did

The process wasn't linear. It was cycles between:

  1. Domain research — SysML v2 spec, symbol conventions, how engineers use it. AI helped translate.
  2. Framework synthesis — Cognitive research (visual-spatial thinking, Bertin's visual variables, Tufte's information design). AI helped cross-reference.
  3. Expert validation — Regular check-ins with the engineering collaborator. Human judgment only.
  4. Slower processing — Pencil and paper. Let it sink in. This can't be accelerated.

The output: a set of design principles categorized by what's non-negotiable (architectural decisions), what's a strong default (configurable), what's a genuine design choice (two valid options), and what's valuable but optional (later version). This taxonomy emerged from synthesizing the frameworks — none of the source fields provided it directly.

Takeaways

For designers working in specialized domains: You can't apply consumer UX rules blindly. You need enough domain understanding to know what's fixed and what's flexible. AI can accelerate this learning — but validation with experts can't be skipped.

For engineers working with designers: "Just do the visual part" underestimates what structural design requires. The visual layer isn't separate from functionality — it emerges from understanding how all parts connect from the user's perspective.

For anyone using AI in professional work: AI is good at translation and synthesis. It's bad at judgment and validation. The risk is generating volume without digestion. The skill is knowing when to accelerate and when to slow down.
