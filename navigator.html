⬡ Navigator · Sound AI Regulations App & Guide

Tools helping artists, labels, and music-tech companies navigate AI regulations

2025–26 · EU AI Act, GDPR, European Accessibility Act · Music, soundscapes, sound design

Research Question

How can artists, platforms, and music-tech companies navigate AI compliance — understanding what's legal, what's required, and how to work ethically in a landscape where AI is here to stay?

Context

AI in sound — music, soundscapes, sound design — isn't going away. Creators worry about consent and compensation. Ethical platforms and new AI-native companies want to do things right. Both need the same thing: clarity on regulations, risks, and how to protect and prove authenticity.

Current resources are either too legal (inaccessible) or too simplified (incomplete). This project addresses both with a bidirectional tool — artists can check if their work was used without consent; companies can verify their AI pipeline is compliant.

Why I built this: Having worked at Valispace — a requirements compliance tool for the aerospace industry — I understood how regulated industries approach compliance tooling. Sound creators and music-tech companies now face similar complexity with EU AI regulations, but lack accessible tools.

Problem Validation

Before designing, I spoke with musicians and observed industry patterns to understand pain points on both sides.

Creator concerns
"No pay, no say"
Artists worry about their sound and lyrics being used to train AI models without consent or compensation. They need to know their rights and when to act.
Platform opportunities
Compliance as a feature
Platforms receiving music submissions could provide compliance guidance to creators. Indie and open-source platforms may want to differentiate AI vs. human work, or integrate auto-updating regulation checks into their workflow.

Market context: 80% of musicians report being "worried" about AI in music. Meanwhile, companies face the EU AI Act, GDPR, and accessibility requirements with no clear guidance. Both sides need orientation. (Sources: PRS For Music, 2026; UK IPO consultation, 2026)

Use Cases

  • Consent & Attribution — Was this work used with permission?
  • Training Data Provenance — Where did training data come from?
  • Generated Output Rights — Who owns AI-generated work?
  • Transparency & Disclosure — When must I disclose AI use?
  • Authenticity & Recognition — How can creators prove and protect original work?

Use cases lean creator-side — individual artists typically want guidance for their specific scenario, not a full compliance audit.

Trade-offs & Decisions

Sequencing
Documentation first, then app
Started with the comprehensive Guide as a single source of truth, then built the Navigator for quick access. The Guide ensures accuracy and auditability; the app ensures accessibility.
Scope
EU regulations, global relevance
Scoped to EU regulations (AI Act, GDPR, Accessibility Act) for V1. The questions around sound, AI, and copyright are global — but legal frameworks differ. Started with EU as a focused, useful baseline.
Architecture
Decision tree, not chatbot
Chose a structured decision tree over an AI chatbot. Users need predictable, verifiable answers for compliance — not probabilistic responses. Every path can be traced and audited.
Positioning
Orientation, not legal advice
Navigator helps users understand if they need to act and when to consult a lawyer. It doesn't replace legal counsel — it helps people know when they need it.

Process

Guide — Document-as-Code

Wrote compliance documentation in Markdown, managed as a linked knowledge base in Obsidian. This enabled version control, cross-referencing between regulations, and easy updates as laws evolve. Designed final PDF in Apple Pages.

Navigator — Prototyping with AI

Prototyped the React app using Claude as a design tool — a fast way to test decision-tree logic and get a working interface for feedback. This let me validate the concept before involving engineering resources.

Handover & Collaboration

Prototype handed over to a backend engineer for professional implementation. Ongoing collaboration on: tool logic and decision-tree structure, auto-updating regulations from official sources, accessibility and language translation (my responsibility).

AI-Human Collaboration

AI-assisted: requirement gathering, cross-verification across models (Claude, Grok), rapid prototyping.

Human-led: design direction, information architecture, accessibility review, editorial decisions, source verification, engineering collaboration.

Outcome & Next Steps

Published as an open-source resource under version control. Prototype validated the concept; now in professional development.

  • V2 in development: Backend engineer building production version with auto-updating regulation feeds from official sources — designed to be embeddable for platforms wanting to offer compliance guidance to their users
  • My ongoing role: Frontend collaboration, accessibility implementation, language translation

Learning and creating in parallel — fusing form and function, testing in real scenarios faster, without over-designing or validating with the wrong audience. Easier to gather feedback online, through data, at scale, and with individual users once identified. This is product-focused work, not only UX — human-focused. The job is changing.
