Aerospace · Traceability Design

A system-in-systems feature for tracing who did what, when, and what changed.

March 2021 – April 2023 · Valispace → Altium 365

Context

In regulated aerospace work, a requirement isn't a line item. It links to hardware, test plans, verification steps, and other requirements across the system. When something changes, the impact propagates — and someone has to know where, and whether the work downstream is still valid.

The feature set had to surface that network: linking requirements across hierarchies, tracing what verifies what, and signalling draft versus reviewed versus final across collaborators. Prototyped 2021–2022, before LLMs entered day-to-day workflows. The problems it addresses — provenance, change propagation, version state across distributed work — have only become more relevant since.

Role

Sole designer. Built the high-fidelity interactive Figma prototypes from scratch — prototype design, interaction, and system logic end-to-end, with occasional review from engineers.

Ran two iteration rounds with external and internal stakeholders. In round two, I pushed to introduce detailed user stories alongside the design, and to define MVP-critical scope explicitly — separating what had to exist on day one from what needed to be extensible later. Not standard practice at the time. Both were my call.

Also contributed to API design consistency and product documentation — reviewed interfaces, flagged inconsistencies, gave feedback on clarity and annotations.

The real bottleneck

Engineering capacity wasn't the constraint. Neither was expertise. The constraint was translation — turning what domain experts knew by reflex into something a developer outside that field could build.

Experts who have worked in regulated aerospace for decades rarely articulate the constraints they've internalized. Much of the work was surfacing assumptions no one questioned anymore, then making them explicit enough to hand across roles. Writing user stories that specified for a novice what an expert does by habit fell to design, not to the experts.

This is a pattern in B2B software at the edge of a domain: the hard part isn't whether the tool can be built, it's whether what the tool should do can be captured as a coherent whole — in language and visuals — before it re-enters the expert's field as a product.
